Theorem 1.9.7. Differentiable implies continuous.

If \(f\) is differentiable at \(z_0\text{,}\) then \(f\) is continuous at \(z_0\text{.}\)

Proof.

Note that by definition, \(z_0\) is assumed to be an interior point of the domain of \(f\text{,}\) which implies that \(z_0\) is a limit point of the domain. We are thus justified in using the limit definition of continuity. We have
\begin{align*} \lim\limits_{z\to z_0}f(z) \amp = \lim\limits_{z\to z_0}\left(\frac{f(z)-f(z_0)}{z-z_0}\cdot (z-z_0)+f(z_0)) \\ \amp = f'(z_0)(0)+f(z_0)=f(z_0)\text{.} \end{align*}
Thus \(f\) is continuous at \(z_0\text{.}\)
