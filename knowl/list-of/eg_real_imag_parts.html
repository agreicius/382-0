Example 1.6.5. Real and imaginary parts.

Let \(f\colon \C\rightarrow \C\) be defined as \(f(z)=z^2\text{.}\)
  1. Find the real and imaginary parts of \(f\text{.}\)
  2. Decide whether \(f\) is injective.
  3. Compute \(\im f=f(\C)\text{.}\)
Solution.
  1. Letting \(z=x+iy\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{align*} f(z) \amp =z^2\\ \amp = (x^2-y^2)+i(2xy)\text{,} \end{align*}
    and thus
    \begin{align*} u(x,y) \amp = x^2-y^2 \amp v(x,y)=2xy\text{.} \end{align*}
  2. Since \(f(-z)=f(z)=z^2\) for all \(z\text{,}\) we see that \(f\) is not injective: as a concrete counterexample, \(f(-1)=f(1)=1\text{.}\)
  3. According to TheoremÂ 1.3.8, every complex number \(w\) has a square-root. Thus for all \(w\in \C\) there is a \(z\in \C\) such that \(f(z)=w\text{.}\) This proves \(f\) is surjective.
