Example 1.17.11. Power series for \(\Log\).

Find a power series expansion of \(-\Log(1-z)\) that converges on \(B_1(0)\text{.}\)
Solution.
Let \(f(z)=1/(1-z)\text{.}\) Since \(f\) is holomorphic on \(B_1(0)\text{,}\) we know from the proof of Theorem 1.14.2 that we can define an antiderivative of \(f\) on \(B_1(0)\) via the formula
\begin{equation*} F(z)=\int_{\gamma_{0,z}}f(w)\, dw\text{,} \end{equation*}
where \(\gamma_{0,z}\) is the straight line parametrization. Using (2) of Theorem 1.17.9, we then have
\begin{align*} F(z) \amp =\int_{\gamma_{0,z}}f(w)\, dw\\ \amp = \int_{\gamma_{0,z}}\sum_{n=0}^\infty w^n\, dw\\ \amp = \sum_{n=0}^\infty\int_{\gamma_{0,z}} w^n\, dw \amp (\knowl{./knowl/xref/th_power_series_integral_der.html}{\text{1.17.9}})\\ \amp =\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{z^{n+1}}{n+1} \text{,} \end{align*}
and that this power series converges for all \(z\in B_1(0)\text{.}\) Note that the last step in the chain of equalities above follows from the fact that \(G(w)=\frac{w^{n+1}}{n+1}\) is an antiderivative of \(w^n\text{,}\) and thus
\begin{equation*} \int_{\gamma_{0,z}} w^n\, dw=G(z)-G(0)=\frac{z^{n+1}}{n+1}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Lastly, since \(F\) and \(-\Log(1-z)\) are both antiderivatives of \(f\) on \(B_1(0)\) (check this for yourself for \(-\Log(1-z)\)), we have \(-\Log(1-z)=F(z)+C\) for some \(C\text{.}\) Evaluating at \(z=0\text{,}\) we see that since \(-\Log(1-0)=F(0)=0\text{,}\) we must have \(C=0\text{.}\) We conclude that
\begin{equation*} -\Log(1-z)=F(z)=\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{n+1}z^{n+1}=z+\frac{z^2}{2}+\frac{z^3}{3}+\cdots \end{equation*}
for all \(z\in B_1(0)\text{.}\)
